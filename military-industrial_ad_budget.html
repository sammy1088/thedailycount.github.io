The Military-Industrial Ad Budget
by Samuel Shreder, posted on 10/23/13

Browsing through the Washington Post, I found an advertisement for a brand new combat warship. Given the ship’s $500 million plus price tag it’s safe to assume that I, an “in-between jobs” 25 year old, was not Lockheed and Martin’s intended audience. I guess I could lobby my congressman to buy one, right? After all, it looks like it was designed for a videogame and of course I want our Navy to be at the cutting edge of technology. Here, take a tour.

Upon digging a little deeper, I found the Washington Post had itself written about this strange, new phenomenon of our military-industrial complex, i.e. private companies promoting their military hardware throughout Washington DC’s subways systems in hopes of influencing that ten or twelve congressional members that actually have a say in the matter. It’s boggling to think how such a messy advertising strategy can be a winning proposition for those private companies, but it must be, and it is particularly so to think that the money used to fuel these messy, million dollar ad campaigns were funded, in part, by tax dollars from their last government contract.

Much like handouts given to insurance companies to help pass the Affordable Healthcare Act, or a bailout to reckless, greedy bankers who then used the loot to give themselves raises, the delegation of services vital to public welfare from the government to private industry can get wildly inefficient and often unethical in the storm of intermingling interests and revolving cast of politicians-turned-lobbyists and lobbyists-turned-politicians.

Given our increasingly untenable debt, austerity measures are unavoidable for the US at this point. Many on the right are calling for social welfare programs to be the first cut, but perhaps we should look to our nation’s ever-increasing $530 billion dollar defense budget before we start cutting food stamps, because discretion is being abused and money spent sloppily when private companies advertise military hardware to the general public. The least they can do is keep this sort of thing behind closed doors…
